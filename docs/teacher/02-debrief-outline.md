# Star Bite Diner — Debrief deck outline

**Time:** 10–15 minutes after the last round.

This is the Markdown source for the debrief slide deck. To make the actual deck:
- Copy each slide's content into [Google Slides](https://slides.google.com)
- Export as PDF when done, save as `02-debrief-deck.pdf` in this folder
- Or use it directly as a teacher's outline / classroom whiteboard plan

---

## Slide 1 — "What just happened?"

**Title:** What just happened?
**Subtitle:** Let's talk about what we just did.

**Visual:** screenshot of the Star Bite Diner game (the lobby, or a screenshot of the customer-result animation) on the right.

**Speaker notes:** Pause for a moment. Let students sit with the round before diving in. Don't ask questions yet — just transition.

---

## Slide 2 — Robots learn from examples

**Title:** Robots learn from examples.

**Body bullets:**
- When you labeled a burger as "rare," the bot remembered.
- When someone gave wrong labels, the bot got worse.
- **That's how real AI works.**

**Bottom callout:** ChatGPT, your phone's camera, YouTube's recommendations — they all learned from examples that real people gave them.

**Visual:** split image. Left = the in-game labeling UI. Right = a screenshot of any familiar AI tool (YouTube homepage, ChatGPT prompt box, photo-tag suggestions).

---

## Slide 3 — Discussion: "What did wrong labels look like?"

**Title:** What did wrong labels look like?

**Two big-text questions:**
- "How could you tell when something was off?"
- "Did one wrong label ruin everything? Or was it more about the pattern over time?"

**Speaker notes:** This is a discussion slide. Wait for at least 3 student responses before moving on. Don't rush this. Listen for: "the accuracy bar dropped," "I noticed someone lingered weird," "we had to call a meeting." If they bring up specific stations, follow up.

---

## Slide 4 — Bad data ≠ broken AI

**Title:** The bot wasn't dumb. It was reflecting what it was taught.

**Subhead:** In real life, AI gives bad answers because the EXAMPLES it learned from were bad — not because the AI itself is stupid.

**Visual:** split panel. Left = a happy 🔥 Grill Bot with a bunch of correct labels around it. Right = a confused 🔥 Grill Bot with a mix of correct and wrong labels.

**Caption under the visual:** Same bot. Different teachers.

---

## Slide 5 — Discussion: "Where do you think real AI gets its examples from?"

**Title:** Where do you think real AI gets its examples from?

**Body:** mostly empty — invite responses.

**Speaker notes:**
- Let students brainstorm 1–2 minutes.
- Reveal answers as they come up: from people typing things on the internet. From things people uploaded and labeled. From customer service chats. From photos on social media.
- Sometimes from **sloppy** or **biased** sources.
- Follow-up: "If the examples were sloppy or biased — what would happen to the AI?"

---

## Slide 6 — Auditing the data (the Review station)

**Title:** Someone has to look at the data.

**Body bullets:**
- The 🔍 Review station in the game? **That's a real job.**
- Real ML teams audit datasets for problems before training the model.
- It's not always fun. It's not glamorous. **But it's how AI doesn't go off the rails.**

**Visual:** a screenshot of the in-game Review station UI showing a list of recent labels with the 🚩 flag button.

---

## Slide 7 — Vocabulary

**Title:** Now let's name what we just learned.

**Body:** clean list of vocabulary words students should add to their AI vocabulary. Each in its own colored card / box.

| Term | What it means |
| --- | --- |
| **Training data** | The examples we give an AI to learn from. |
| **Label** | The "answer" attached to an example (e.g., "this is a banana peel; it's compost"). |
| **Model** | What the AI builds from training data. It's how it makes decisions. |
| **Data poisoning** | Putting bad examples in on purpose, to make the AI worse. |
| **Dataset auditing** | Checking the training data for problems. |
| **Ground truth** | The actual right answer, against which we judge the AI's output. |
| **Probabilistic** | The AI doesn't always get it right. It has odds, like a weighted coin flip. |

---

## Slide 8 — "Where does this matter?"

**Title:** What's an AI you use that might have been trained on bad examples?

**Body:** open question, blank space for student responses.

**Speaker notes (don't show students yet — let them go first):**
- Let students share 2–3 examples first.
- Seeds if students are quiet:
  - Face filters that don't work as well on darker skin
  - Autocomplete that suggests biased words
  - Image generators that get certain kinds of people wrong
  - Voice assistants that don't understand certain accents
  - Recommendation algorithms that show kids weird videos
- **Closing question:** "How would you 'audit' an AI you use? What would you check?"

---

## Optional Slide 9 — "What we'd do differently next time"

If you have an extra 2 minutes, ask:
- "What would you do differently if you played another round as a trainer?"
- "What would you do differently as a saboteur?"

This is a low-stakes way to get students to articulate WHAT THEY LEARNED about how the bots work. It also makes them want to play again.

---

## Closing

End with: "**The robots aren't smart or dumb. They're a mirror of what we teach them.** That's true for real AI too. Pay attention to what gets used to teach them — and what doesn't."

---

## Tone reminders

- Warm, friendly, slightly silly.
- Speak to students like capable people.
- The bot is **never** the enemy. It's the team's trainee.
- Mistakes are framed as "the bot got confused," not "you failed."
- Saboteurs are **goofy mischief**, not menace.
